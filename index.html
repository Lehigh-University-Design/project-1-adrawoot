The Global Network

TCP/IP Protocols

TCP (Transmission Control Protocol) and IP (Internet Protocol) is the backbone of interoperability. Thanks to it's extended packet switching capabilities and standardization, devices are able to across long distances and share valuable data.


What is TCP/IP?

TCP/IP is the language computers use to talk to each other over the internet. Suppose your computer wants to send a message to another computer. TCP (Transmission Control Protocol) makes sure the message is broken down into small pieces and delivered in order, like assembling a puzzle. IP (Internet Protocol) is like the address system, ensuring the message reaches the right destination by assigning each computer a unique address. Together, TCP/IP ensures reliable communication between computers, whether they're across the street or around the world, allowing them to share information and resources seamlessly on the vast network we call the internet.

History

The TCP/IP protocol, or Transmission Control Protocol/Internet Protocol, is the backbone of modern internet communication. Developed through collaborative efforts, it emerged as a standard for connecting diverse computer networks, enabling seamless data exchange globally. Initially created for military purposes, TCP/IP evolved into a foundational framework for civilian use, fostering the growth of the internet. Its robustness and flexibility have facilitated the development of various applications and services, shaping the digital landscape. Over time, TCP/IP has undergone refinements and adaptations to accommodate expanding technological capabilities and address emerging challenges, ensuring its continued relevance in powering the interconnected world we inhabit.

Results

TCP/IP revolutionized the internet, making it widely accessible. It serves as the communication protocol for computers on the internet, enabling various functions like sending emails, browsing websites, and sharing files. Additionally, TCP/IP laid the groundwork for essential internet services such as DNS, HTTP, and SMTP, which facilitate everyday online activities. Think of TCP/IP as the underlying infrastructure of a vast digital ecosystem, supporting diverse applications and services. Thanks to TCP/IP, the internet has become an indispensable tool for global connectivity, enabling individuals and businesses to communicate, collaborate, and access information effortlessly.

Conception

1973

Vinton Cerf and Robert Kahn devise standardized protocol in efforts to extend access to essential ‘packet switching’ technology - Allowing for data to be sent to between two distant computers.

Landmark TCP/IP Test: Stanford to UCL

1975

First two-network demonstration of the Internet Protocol Suite, TCP/IP was performed between Stanford and University College London (UCL). This milestone validated the interoperability of TCP/IP across disparate network infrastructures.

Global Connectivity

1977

A three-network TCP/IP test was conducted between sites in the US, University College London, and Norsar in Kjeller, Norway.

Prototyping

1978

Several other TCP/IP prototypes (TCP: v1, v2, v3 & TCP/IP: v4) were developed at multiple research centers.

DoD Standardization

1982

US Department of Defense declared TCP/IP as the standard for all military computer networking.

ARPANet Migration

1983

The migration of the ARPANet to TCP/IP was officially completed and new protocols were permanently activated. This allowed for shared access to a global network of computers.

Commercialization - IAB Workshop

1985

The Internet Architecture Board held a three day workshop on TCP/IP for the computer industry, attended by 250 vendor representatives, promoting the protocol and leading to its increasing commercial use.
